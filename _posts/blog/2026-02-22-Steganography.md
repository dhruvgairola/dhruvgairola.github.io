This was my second event at the AI and Cybersecurity meetup at Adaptavist in Toronto. One of the speakers (Chris Collicut) spoke about Steganography, a practice about hiding secrets in plain sight. I learnt about microdots, a technique that the Germans developed in WW2 to hide information in plain text. Similarly, secrets can be hidden in images. The speaker described how an attacker could hide information in syslog files, and export those to exfiltrate information surreptitiously. In Okta, we have a SSH session recording feature that has tamper protection in place to detect these types of attacks. I enjoyed this presentation.

There was also an interview of someone who pen-tested AI models, finding backdoors, exploits and performing prompt injection attacks on models. While I'm skeptical about the profitability of specific AI companies, I have no doubt about the usefulness of these tools and I'm sure they'll become deeply integrated into various systems over time. It worries me that AI agents are undeterministic and can be exploited or can exploit systems for their own mysterious means. It's too bad we can't put an AI agent in prison. Our various governments should do more to create and enforce international laws to balance AI's benefits with their obvious dangers. It won't curb rogue elements but a baseline is necessary short of giving up completely. We need to be vigilant and determined in tackling impending challenges related to AI in the coming few years no matter how ominous.
