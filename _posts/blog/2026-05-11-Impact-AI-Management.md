Recent trends indicate that organizations are flattening their management structures. Coinbase recently laid off [14% of it's staff](https://fortune.com/2026/05/05/coinbase-layoffs-14-of-employees-ai-tech-ai-job-anxiety-crypto/) and many of those impacted were managers. Those remaining are expected to function as player-coaches, balancing IC duties with traditional people-management. Even in my company, there has been some amount of flattening in order to reduce bureaucracy and enable faster decision making.

A traditional manager typically shifts between multiple roles to help increase their team output. In an [earlier](https://dhruvgairola.com/Leadership-Styles/) post, I wrote that managers have to be visionaries/team-builders/coaches/etc depending on circumstances. A sole emphasis on player-coach constrains a manager because it's harder for them to shift focus. For example, they will have less time to be strategic if they're also coding actively. I view the recent changes as a temporary necessity until the software industry stabilizes. It seems logical that managers should invest into building up AI skills as ICs in order to guide and support their teams through these transitions.

Even before the rise of LLMs, some companies preferred their managers to function as player-coaches exclusively. I have personally interviewed at companies where they expected managers to play a "tech lead" role. Google is an example where a flatter structure has always been the norm. Today, companies want more technically oriented managers likely because AI has made code production easier. Developers have used techniques to make it safer to code with LLMs (e.g., adding more context to LLMs, implementing quality harnesses, skills in the codebase, etc). Innovations (e.g., PRs can be created from messaging tools like Slack) have further reduced barriers and shifted the bottleneck to PR reviews.

Managers are also expected to manage larger teams. An [article](https://fortune.com/2026/04/07/megamanager-era-how-many-direct-reports-ai-middle-management/) calls such people "megamanagers". In 2026, managers have an average of 12 direct reports compared to 6 reports a decade earlier. The flattening or org structures is causing this but also the expectation that AI can make managers more efficient. In theory, if AI can summarize emails, capture meeting minutes, organize schedules, etc, the manager has more time to focus on other duties.

To summarize, AI is increasing pressure on managers to become extremely efficient. Not only do managers have to be player-coaches and write/review code, but they also have more reports due to flatter org structures and AI tools. In order to avoid burnout, managers have to dig deep and get their hands dirty. They have go a level deeper - it's not purely about writing good prompts but also experimenting with MCPs, skills, harnesses, etc. These will help managers guide both themselves and their team. Managers should track newer types of metrics to ensure delivery and quality. AI is changing our industry in deep ways - who thought that tagging a bot in a slack thread could automatically lead to a decent PR? In my opinion, a player-coach manager who focuses on AI learning is an apt role to play until the software industry attains some sort of an "AI-equilibrium".
